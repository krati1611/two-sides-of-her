Two Sides of Her

a flash fiction
I — the free one

She always arrived at the café first — barefoot in sandals even in October, a wildflower tucked behind her ear like punctuation. Her laugh came before she did, spilling through the doorway ahead of her whole body. She carried a journal that smelled of cedar and airport stamps. People noticed her the way you notice a window left open in winter — something alive rushing in.

She talked to strangers about rivers. She had opinions about clouds. She once cried at a sunset not because she was sad, but because she said "beautiful things deserve witnesses." Everyone who knew her called her Lia — short for nothing, long for everything.

II — the sharp one

She walked into rooms like a system update — silent, inevitable, changing everything before you knew it happened. Her laptop never closed. Her eyes were always three thoughts ahead of the conversation. At parties she stood by the speaker, not because she loved the music, but because the bass drowned out small talk and small talk bored her.

She built things. Real things — models that could think, code that could breathe. She wore black like a uniform, kept her coffee black too. Warmth lived in her somewhere, people said, but it was encrypted. Everyone who knew her called her Vera — precise, clean, final.

Lia ✕ Vera
III — the crossings

They kept finding each other in the spaces between worlds.

First, a mountain trail in Lisbon — Lia photographing moss, Vera mapping a hiking route on her phone's GPS. They passed each other without words, only a glance, the kind that lasts exactly one second too long.

Second, a tech conference in Seoul. Lia was the photographer hired to shoot the event; Vera was on stage, mid-keynote, mid-sentence — and she stopped. Just for a breath. Because there was that face again, half-hidden behind a camera lens, laughing at something no one else had noticed.

Third — a rainy night bookstore in Amsterdam. One woman was buying a poetry collection. The other, a book on neural architecture. They reached for the last paper bag at the counter at the exact same moment. Their hands touched. They both pulled back. They both laughed. Different laughs — one like a bell, one like a key turning.

"We keep meeting," Lia said.

Vera tilted her head. "I've been thinking the same thing."

"Strange," said Lia. "I feel like I know you."

"Strange," said Vera softly. "I feel like I am you."

IV — the mirror

The bookstore owner cleared his throat. "You forgot your card, Miss—" He looked between them, confused. "I'm sorry — I only see one name here."

He held out the receipt.

One name. One signature. One birthday printed at the top from the loyalty program.

They stared at the paper. Then at each other. Then — slowly — at the window beside them, where the rain had turned the glass into a mirror.

There was only one woman standing there. And she was everything — the wildflower and the algorithm, the laugh and the silence, the open window and the locked door.

She had always known, somewhere underneath the noise of living, that she was too much for one version of herself. So she had split — given wonder to one half and precision to the other, let them wander the world separately until they were ready to be whole.

She picked up the receipt. Read her own name like a sentence she was finishing after a very long pause.

Outside, the rain softened. She stepped into it — barefoot in sandals, laptop bag on her shoulder, wildflower still tucked behind her ear — and she was beautiful, and she was brilliant, and she was, finally, just herself.
